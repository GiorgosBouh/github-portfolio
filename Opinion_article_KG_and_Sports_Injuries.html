Injury Prediction Is Broken — Here’s How Semantic AI Can Fix It

Posted on April 13, 2025

Years ago, I was training for long jump — a sport I loved deeply. One day during a lift off, I felt something go wrong in my knee. It turned out to be a meniscus tear, and although my ACL wasn’t fully torn, it was likely affected too. That injury changed everything. I couldn’t train the same way again, and eventually, many years later, it pushed me toward a different path — a PhD focused on knee and ACL reconstruction. Since then, I’ve worked in biomechanics, AI, and knowledge graphs. And looking back, I often ask myself: could that injury have been predicted — or even prevented — if we had the right tools? My answer today is yes. But we need a new kind of thinking.

We Have the Data — But Not the Full Picture

We live in a time where we can collect all kinds of movement data: vertical ground reaction forces (vGRF), EMG signals from muscles, joint angles, past injury reports, training loads. But most of these live in different systems, with no real connection between them. What’s even more surprising is that many coaches and teams don’t even store structured evaluation data over time. They run tests, collect numbers, maybe print reports — but a season later, it’s like that data never existed. There’s no real baseline to compare against. And that’s strange, because we now have the tools to monitor an athlete biomechanically across an entire season with minimal effort. Wearables, cloud storage, even mobile EMG systems are widely accessible. The problem isn’t the lack of technology — it’s knowing how to connect and use the data meaningfully. Injuries are almost never caused by a single factor. They’re the result of patterns and interactions — things that build up over time. And without structured, connected data, we miss the chance to see those patterns.

Connecting the Dots with Semantic AI

That’s where knowledge graphs and semantic AI come in. A knowledge graph is a way of organizing information that focuses on relationships. Instead of just storing data, it shows how different factors relate: • This athlete had a meniscus tear. • Their vGRF shows asymmetrical loading. • EMG reveals delayed activation in one muscle. • Training load has been high recently. A system like this can reason through the connections and say: “This athlete is showing a risky pattern. You should take a closer look.” It’s not just prediction. It’s prevention with explanation.

Why It Matters

As someone who’s both lived through an injury and studied them deeply, I see huge potential here. Not only for elite athletes, but also for coaches, physical therapists, and everyday people who want to stay active and healthy.

Imagine being able to spot problems before they turn into something serious — not by guessing, but through intelligent, connected systems that understand the whole picture.

Final Thoughts

We already have the sensors, the data, and the computing power. What we’ve been missing is the structure — a way to link everything together and extract meaning.

Semantic AI can help us do that.
Not just to treat injuries after they happen, but to stop them before they do.
