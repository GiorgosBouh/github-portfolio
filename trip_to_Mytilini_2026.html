AI in Culture and Society: Reflections from Mytilini

Mytilini, 28-29 May 2026

The workshop “AI in Culture and Society”, held in Mytilini on 28 May 2026, brought together speakers whose work helps explain why artificial intelligence is no longer only a technical subject. The talks were important because they connected AI with the ways we study society, culture, human movement, health, and everyday digital life.

The invited speakers approached AI from complementary perspectives. Prof. Giorgos Vouros discussed the current landscape of artificial intelligence, agents, helping frame the opportunities and challenges that shape the field today. Prof. Nikos Stergiou connected AI with biomechanics, showing how intelligent methods can support the study of human movement and variability. His work has been a major source of inspiration for this research direction, and his scientific vision continues to serve as an important guide for us. Prof. N.-Christos Anagnostopoulos focused on the relationship between AI and culture, while Prof. Evangelia Kavakli explored AI in relation to digital well-being.

Together, these presentations showed why interdisciplinary dialogue is essential when working with AI. Technical progress becomes more meaningful when it is discussed alongside its cultural, social, ethical, and human dimensions. The workshop created a space where these dimensions could be considered together, rather than as separate conversations.

The following day, 29 May 2026, I had the opportunity to present part of my postdoctoral research on the use of AI and knowledge graphs in the biomechanics of developmental disorders. My talk focused on how knowledge graph methods can help organise and connect complex information from biomechanics, clinical assessment, developmental profiles, and movement analysis.

In this context, AI is not simply a tool for prediction. It can also support interpretation by linking data, concepts, and domain knowledge in ways that make biomechanical patterns easier to study. This is especially important in developmental disorders, where movement, function, and clinical characteristics are often multidimensional and need to be understood through an integrated research perspective.

Presenting this work after the AI in Culture and Society workshop was particularly meaningful, because it placed my research within a broader discussion about how AI can contribute to science while remaining connected to human needs, social questions, and responsible innovation.

I would like to thank the organisers, speakers, colleagues, and participants for the thoughtful discussions and warm hospitality in Mytilini. The two days offered a valuable opportunity to exchange ideas across disciplines and to reflect on the future role of AI in culture, society, health, and biomechanics. I would especially like to thank Prof. Konstantinos Kotis for all the guidance, the excellent organisation of the workshop and for his generous hospitality.
